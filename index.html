Hands-on practice

Labs

Five labs, each adding a new layer to the Mealify app — from the first layout to a working local database. Each lab shows two screenshots, a video walkthrough, and a short reflection.

Lab 1 · UI

Building the Home Screen

Laid out Mealify's home screen in Jetpack Compose: the category row, banner carousel, and store cards.

Reflection: Getting the spacing and card elevation to feel balanced was harder than expected. It taught me how much a clean visual hierarchy depends on consistent padding and a restrained type scale, and that small layout decisions add up to the overall feel.
Lab 2 · Interaction

Add to Cart & Quantity Stepper

Wired up the behaviour behind the UI: the quantity stepper, the Add to Cart button, and tap feedback on each dish.

Reflection: Connecting state to controls showed me how Compose recomposition keeps the screen in sync. I learned to hoist state into one place so the UI stays predictable, instead of scattering it across components.
Lab 3 · Material Design

Theming with Material 3

Defined Mealify's colour, typography, and shape tokens, including a magenta primary and a fresh-green alternate theme.

Reflection: Being able to change the whole app's look by editing a single theme file made design tokens finally click for me. I now see colour and type as a system to maintain, not decoration to sprinkle on each screen.
Lab 4 · Navigation

Bottom Nav & Detail Screens

Connected screens with Navigation Compose and a bottom bar for Home, Cart, Orders, and Account, passing the selected dish into the detail screen.

Reflection: Handling the back stack and passing arguments was the trickiest part of the course so far. Working through it gave me a real feel for navigation graphs and why a clear screen structure matters.
Lab 5 · Room

Saving the Cart & Order History

Added a Room database to persist the cart and the order history that powers Mealify's "My Activity History" screen.

Reflection: Modelling entities and DAOs and observing data with Flow showed me how local storage keeps an app usable offline. Seeing my orders survive an app restart was the moment the project felt like a real product.

Project 1 · Lab 4 + Working Features

Mealify

Problem statement: Students around Bangi struggle to find affordable meals from nearby kitchens in one place, and small cafes lack an easy channel to sell surplus stock. Mealify brings local stores, deals, and ordering into a single app.
Lessons learned: Building the full order flow taught me to think in features, not just screens. I learned to keep navigation, state, and cart logic decoupled so each could be tested on its own — and that a tidy data model early on saves a lot of refactoring later.

Looking back

Reflection & Learning Journey

I started this course able to sketch a screen but unsure how to make one actually work. Building Mealify lab by lab changed that: I learned to turn a layout into interactive state, connect screens with navigation, and finally persist real data with Room and the cloud. The hardest moments — debugging the navigation back stack and handling failed network calls — ended up teaching me the most, because they forced me to understand what the framework was doing rather than copying snippets. I'm leaving with a working app I'm proud of and the confidence to plan, build, and ship a mobile feature from scratch.
